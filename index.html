Understanding Motion Types

What is a Motion?

A motion is the topic of the debate. It's a statement that one side (the Proposition or Government) must support, and the other side (the Opposition) must oppose. Understanding the specific wording of a motion is the most critical skill in debate, as it dictates what each team has to prove to win. Motions are typically categorized by their phrasing, which sets different burdens on the debaters.

THW: "This House Would..."

These are policy motions. They propose a specific action or plan. The Proposition team has the burden to present a "model"â€”a clear, practical plan explaining how they would implement the motion. The Opposition can either argue against the policy in principle, argue that the practical outcomes will be negative, or propose a counter-model (a different plan to solve the same problem).

Example: "THW ban unpaid internships."
Proposition's Job: Propose a law to ban them, explain who would enforce it, and argue why this leads to a better, fairer world.
Opposition's Job: Argue why this ban is a bad idea (e.g., it reduces opportunities for students) or propose a better alternative (e.g., regulating internships instead of banning them).

THBT: "This House Believes That..."

These are value judgment motions. They ask teams to argue whether a statement is true or false, right or wrong. No model is required. The debate is a philosophical clash over competing values or a factual analysis of the world as it is. The Proposition must prove the statement is, on balance, true, while the Opposition must prove it is, on balance, false.

Example: "THBT social media has done more harm than good."
Proposition's Job: Prove that the negative impacts of social media (e.g., on mental health, democracy) outweigh the positive impacts (e.g., connectivity).
Opposition's Job: Prove that the benefits outweigh the harms.

THR: "This House Regrets..."

These are retrospective motions. They invite teams to evaluate a past event, trend, or decision. The Proposition must argue why the world would be better today if that thing had never happened (the "counterfactual"). The Opposition must defend the status quo, arguing that the world is better with the thing having happened. The debate is a comparison between the world as it is and a hypothetical alternative reality.

Example: "THR the invention of the smartphone."
Proposition's Job: Paint a picture of a world without smartphones and argue that this world would be preferable (e.g., better mental health, more genuine social interaction).
Opposition's Job: Argue that the benefits of smartphones (e.g., access to information, safety) make our current world better than the counterfactual.

TH, as X...: "This House, as X..."

These are actor motions. They force the Proposition team to argue from the perspective of a specific person, group, or institution (e.g., "the feminist movement," "a developing nation"). All arguments must be consistent with the likely interests, values, and capabilities of that actor. The Opposition argues against the motion, but does not have to adopt a specific persona. These motions test a debater's ability to analyze stakeholder incentives deeply.

Example: "TH, as the environmentalist movement, would support nuclear energy."
Proposition's Job: Argue that, from the perspective of someone whose primary goal is to stop climate change, supporting nuclear energy is the most logical and effective strategy.
Opposition's Job: Argue that the environmentalist movement should not support nuclear energy (e.g., due to risks of waste, meltdown, or better alternatives).

THS / THC: "This House Supports / Condemns..."

These are similar to THBT motions as they are value-based. "Supports" motions require the Proposition to align themselves with a particular movement, ideology, or action and defend it. "Condemns" motions require the Proposition to stand against something. These are debates about principles and whether it is morally or strategically right to offer support or condemnation.

Example: "THS the use of civil disobedience to achieve political change."
Proposition's Job: Argue that civil disobedience is a legitimate and effective tool for progress.
Opposition's Job: Argue that civil disobedience is illegitimate, counter-productive, or that there are better ways to achieve change.

WSDC Scoring Rubric

The Three Categories of Scoring

In the World Schools Debating Championships (WSDC) format, judges assess speakers on three distinct categories for substantive speeches (the first three speakers on each team). The Reply Speech is scored holistically. Understanding these categories is key to improving as a debater, as it shows you exactly what judges are looking for.

Content

40

The arguments themselves. This includes the logic of your points, the evidence you provide, and the relevance of your material to the motion.

Style

40

How you speak. This covers your use of language, vocal tone and pace, eye contact, gestures, and overall persuasive delivery.

Strategy

20

The structure and choices you make. This includes the organization of your speech, time management, and your engagement with the debate's key issues.

Scoring the Reply Speech (out of 50)

The Reply Speech is not scored on the three separate categories. Instead, it receives a single, holistic score that is half of a substantive speech score. A good reply speech (35/50) identifies the one or two key points of clash in the debate and persuasively explains why their team won those clashes. It is a biased summary, not a rebuttal speech, and should tell a compelling story of the debate from their side's perspective.

Debate Formats

Understanding Debate Formats

While the core principles of argumentation are universal, different debate formats have unique rules, roles, and strategies. This guide is primarily based on the World Schools Debating Championships (WSDC) format, as its 3-vs-3 structure and focus on clear, substantive debate provide a strong foundation for most other styles.

World Schools (WSDC)

A 3-vs-3 format where teams (Proposition and Opposition) have three speakers each giving 8-minute speeches. It emphasizes clear argumentation, engagement through POIs, and a holistic summary of the debate's key clashes. A key feature is the 4-minute Reply Speech, given by either the first or second speaker at the end of the debate, which frames the debate from their side's perspective.

British Parliamentary (BP)

A 4-team format with two teams on each side (Opening Government, Closing Government, Opening Opposition, Closing Opposition). Each team has two speakers. The challenge lies in defeating the opposing side while also distinguishing your team from your partner team on the same bench. BP requires strategic extensions and nuanced weighing.

American Parliamentary (AP)

A 2-vs-2 format with a Prime Minister (PM), Member of Opposition (MO), Minister of the Crown (MC), and Leader of the Opposition (LO). It features different speech times and a strong emphasis on rhetoric, framework debates, and direct refutation within a fast-paced structure.

1st Proposition

The Architect. Your job is to set up the debate. You must clearly define the motion, present your team's model (if needed), and lay out your case. You will use CPR to frame the debate and present 1-2 clear arguments using the PEEL structure. Your speech should be clear, confident, and leave no doubt about what your team stands for.

1st Opposition

The Architect of the Opposition. Your first job is to clash directly with the 1st Prop by rebutting their case setup and arguments. Then, you must present your own constructive case, often with its own CPR framing, and deliver your own arguments using PEEL. You must be both a rebuilder and a builder.

2nd Proposition & 2nd Opposition

The Builder & The Brawler. These speakers have two jobs. First, they must defend their own case and rebut the arguments made by the previous speaker. This is crucial for staying in the debate. Second, they must introduce new arguments to extend their case and add depth. These speeches are about both defense and offense.

3rd Proposition & 3rd Opposition

The Analyst. The third speakers do not introduce new arguments (unless explicitly flagged by their first speaker, which is rare and risky). Their entire speech is dedicated to rebuttal and analysis. Your job is to identify the main Points of Clash in the debate and explain why your team has won them. This requires you to listen carefully to the entire debate and synthesize the key issues.

Reply Speeches (Opp then Prop)

The Storyteller. This is a 4-minute speech given by the 1st or 2nd speaker. It's a biased summary of the debate from your side's perspective. You should not introduce new arguments or rebuttal. Instead, you should frame the debate around the key clashes and explain why, based on the arguments made, your team's vision of the world is more compelling and should win.

CPR: Context, Problem, Resolution

What is CPR?

CPR is your first step in prep time. It's a brainstorming framework that forces you to define the debate on your terms. By establishing the Context, identifying a clear Problem, and proposing a direct Resolution, you build a coherent case with a strong narrative from the very beginning. Spend the first 2-3 minutes of prep on this.

Context

This is the "reality" of the debate. What is happening in the world right now that makes this motion relevant? You need to paint a picture for the judge. Key questions: What is the status quo? What are the key facts and trends? Why are we debating this now?

Problem

Within your context, what is the specific harm or injustice that needs to be fixed? Who is the primary victim? Why is their suffering significant? A clear, compelling problem gives your case a moral core and a clear purpose.

Resolution

This is your team's ultimate goal or aim. For problem-based motions (like "THW..."), your resolution is how you solve the problem. For comparative motions (like "THR..." or "THBT..."), your resolution is the better world you are advocating for. This is the core idea that every argument you make should link back to in the PEEL framework.

Good vs. Bad CPR

THW ban unpaid internships.

Good Example

C: In a world of rising tuition and living costs, access to career-launching internships is increasingly limited to wealthy students who can afford to work for free. P: This creates a structural barrier for talented, low-income students, entrenching generational inequality and robbing industries of diverse perspectives. R: Banning unpaid internships ensures that entry-level opportunities are accessible to all, based on merit, not financial background.

Bad Example

C: Internships are a thing companies offer. P: It's unfair that some people don't get paid. R: We would ban them so everyone gets paid.

(Why it's bad: Vague context, undefined problem, and a simplistic resolution that doesn't explain the mechanism or impact.)

PEEL: Point, Explain, Example, Link

What is PEEL?

PEEL is the universal structure for a strong, persuasive argument. It's the atomic unit of debate. Following this structure ensures your points are clear, logical, and impactful, guiding the judge through your reasoning step-by-step and making it hard to refute.

Point

State your argument in a single, clear, and concise sentence. This is the headline for your argument.

Explain

Provide the logical reason why your point is true. This is the "mechanism" of your argument. Ask yourself "why?" multiple times to deepen your analysis. Explain the incentives of actors and the chain of cause and effect.

Example

Give a specific, real-world example or a well-reasoned hypothetical that illustrates your explanation in action. An example doesn't prove your point, but it makes your explanation more plausible and memorable.

Link

Connect your argument back to the core question of the debate. Explain why this specific point is important and how it helps your side win. This is where you connect back to your team's overall aim, as defined in the Resolution of your CPR.

Good vs. Bad PEEL

THBT standardized testing is more harmful than beneficial.

Good Example

P: Standardized testing perpetuates educational inequality. E: Because these tests are expensive to prepare for, wealthy families can afford extensive tutoring and prep courses, giving their children a significant advantage. This transforms the test from a measure of aptitude into a measure of wealth. E: For instance, the SAT has been shown to have a strong correlation with family income; students from the highest income brackets consistently score hundreds of points higher than those from the lowest. L: Therefore, by relying on these tests, we are not creating a meritocracy, but are actively entrenching the privileges of the wealthy, which is the most significant harm in a debate about fairness in education.

Bad Example

P: Standardized tests are bad. E: They don't really test intelligence. E: The SAT is a bad test. L: So we should get rid of them.

(Why it's bad: The point is a vague assertion, the explanation lacks a mechanism, the example is just a name-drop, and the link has no weighing.)

Stakeholder Analysis

What is Stakeholder Analysis?

Debates are about people. Stakeholder analysis is the process of identifying the key groups affected by a motion and analyzing their interests and incentives. This is one of the best ways to find the most important arguments in a round.

How to Use a Stakeholder Matrix

A stakeholder matrix helps you organize your thoughts. For each stakeholder, you should identify their current situation, their core interests, how the motion impacts them, and why that impact is important.

The NILE Framework

What is NILE?

NILE helps you build a diverse and robust case by prompting four different kinds of arguments. Instead of making four similar points, NILE pushes you to argue from different philosophical angles, making your case more resilient and comprehensive.

Necessity

Why must we act now? This argument establishes urgency. It proves that the status quo is not just bad, but actively harmful or getting worse. It answers the "why now?" question, making the debate feel important.

Impact

What are the long-term consequences? This focuses on the scale and significance of the outcome. It explains how your policy will lead to the best possible future by creating broad, positive changes for society, the economy, or the environment.

Legitimacy

What principle justifies this? This is an argument from moral philosophy. It claims your side is right based on a core value like fairness, autonomy, rights, or justice, regardless of the practical outcomes. It shows your side is morally consistent.

Effectiveness

How will this change behavior? This is a practical argument about incentives. It explains how the motion will work in the real world by altering the behavior of key stakeholders in a predictable and beneficial way. It proves your plan is plausible.

NILE Example

THBT social media has done more harm than good.

  • Necessity: The current trajectory of teen mental health, which is strongly correlated with social media use, constitutes a crisis that demands immediate action to avert further harm.
  • Impact: In the long term, the algorithmic promotion of outrage over nuance erodes the shared factual reality required for a functioning democracy, an much more important impact than any connective benefits.
  • Legitimacy: The business model of social media, predicated on harvesting vast amounts of personal data without meaningful consent, is a fundamental violation of the right to privacy.
  • Effectiveness: The platforms are designed to be addictive, changing human behavior to prioritize short-term validation over genuine connection, which is a predictable and harmful effect of their core mechanics.

MICE Incentives

What is MICE?

MICE is a framework for analyzing why individuals and groups act the way they do. When you analyze a stakeholder, you can use MICE to predict their behavior by understanding their core motivations. This makes your analysis deeper and more persuasive.

Money

The drive for financial gain, economic security, and material well-being. This is often the most straightforward incentive for corporations and individuals in economic debates.

Ideology

The drive to act in accordance with one's beliefs, values, and worldview. This can be political, religious, or moral, and often explains actions that seem to run counter to material self-interest.

Coercion

The drive to act based on fear or external pressure. An actor is coerced when they act to avoid a negative consequence, such as punishment, social ostracism, or physical harm.

Ego

The drive for personal fulfillment, respect, pride, and a sense of purpose. This isn't just about arrogance; it's about the deep human need to feel valued and maintain a positive self-image.

MICE Example

THW hold social media platforms legally liable for harmful user-generated content.

Analysis of a Social Media CEO's incentives:

  • Money: The CEO's primary incentive is to maximize profit. They will resist this motion because content moderation at the scale required is extremely expensive and would reduce profits. Their compensation is tied to stock performance, which is tied to user engagement, and controversial content is often highly engaging.
  • Ideology: The CEO may genuinely believe in a libertarian interpretation of "free speech," arguing that their platform is a neutral public square and should not be the arbiter of truth. They will frame their opposition to the motion as a defense of this principle.
  • Coercion: If the law passes, the CEO will be forced to invest heavily in moderation to avoid massive fines and lawsuits. The fear of legal and financial punishment becomes a powerful coercive incentive to change their behavior and police their platform.
  • Ego: The CEO's self-image is tied to being seen as an innovator and a champion of free expression. They will be motivated to fight the motion to protect this legacy and avoid the public perception of being a censor or a purveyor of harmful content.

Rebuttal

What is Rebuttal?

Rebuttal is the art of dismantling your opponent's arguments. It's not enough to present your own case; you must also show why the other side is wrong. Good rebuttal directly engages with the opponent's logic and proves it to be flawed, irrelevant, or less important than your own points.

How to Structure Rebuttal

Use a simple, three-step process for every rebuttal point:

  1. "They said..." - State their argument clearly and concisely.
  2. "But..." - Explain exactly why their argument is wrong. (e.g., logical flaw, incorrect fact, mischaracterization).
  3. "Therefore..." - Explain the implication. Why does this rebuttal matter? How does it weaken their case and strengthen yours?

Good vs. Bad Rebuttal

Opponent's Argument: "A tax on sugary drinks is regressive and unfairly harms the poor."

Good Rebuttal

"They said this tax unfairly harms the poor. But this argument ignores that the health consequences of obesity and diabetes, like lost wages and high medical bills, are also disproportionately borne by the poor. While our policy has a short-term financial cost, their world locks the poor into a long-term cycle of poor health and poverty. Therefore, our policy is actually more progressive because it addresses a root cause of inequality, making it the more compassionate choice."

Bad Rebuttal

"They said this hurts the poor, but that's not true. Poor people should just drink healthier things. This argument is wrong."

(Why it's bad: It's an assertion, not an argument. It dismisses the point without engaging its logic and offers no counter-analysis or weighing.)

Points of Information (POIs)

What are POIs?

A Point of Information is a short question or statement (under 15 seconds) offered to an opponent during their speech. They are a crucial tool for engagement. You should aim to offer several POIs during each opponent's speech and accept 1-2.

Types of POIs

  • Clarification: Ask for a definition or detail if the speaker is being vague. "On that, could you clarify what you mean by 'a significant investment'?"
  • Challenge: Directly question the logic or evidence of their point. "On that, if your plan is so effective, why has it failed in the real-world example of X?"
  • Contradiction: Point out an inconsistency with something their partner said. "On that, your partner said this policy was about freedom, but you're arguing it's about safety. Which is it?"
  • Setup: Ask a seemingly innocent question that sets up an argument your partner will make later. "On that, would you agree that the primary duty of a state is to protect its most vulnerable citizens?"

Good vs. Bad POIs

Context: An opponent is arguing that prisons successfully rehabilitate criminals.

Good POI

"Given the extremely high rates of re-offense for former prisoners, on what basis can you claim the system is rehabilitating anyone?"

(Why it's good: It's short, sharp, and uses a fact to directly challenge the premise of their argument, forcing them to defend their logic.)

Bad POI

"Don't you think that's wrong? Because there are lots of studies that show that when people get out of prison they just commit more crimes because they can't get a job and society rejects them, so the whole system is a failure."

(Why it's bad: It's a long, rambling speech, not a question. The speaker will simply wave it down and continue their speech uninterrupted.)

Points of Clash

What are Clashes?

Clashes are the main points of disagreement in the debate. They are the fundamental questions that the debate boils down to. Identifying these clashes allows you to structure your later speeches (especially the summary/whip speech) around the most important issues, showing the judge why you win the arguments that matter most.

How to Win Clashes with Weighing

Once you identify a clash, you need to prove you've won it. You do this through weighing: directly comparing your impacts to your opponent's and showing why yours are more significant. Common weighing mechanisms include:

  • Magnitude/Scale: Our impact affects more people.
  • Severity: Our impact is more intense or harmful/beneficial for those it affects.
  • Likelihood: Our impact is more certain to happen than their speculative one.
  • Timeframe: Our impact happens sooner, addressing an urgent problem.
  • Vulnerability: Our impact helps or protects a group that needs special consideration.

Clash Example

THW implement a Universal Basic Income (UBI).

After the first few speeches, two major clashes have emerged:

  1. The Clash of Economic Impact: The Government argues UBI stimulates the economy and reduces poverty. The Opposition argues it creates massive inflation and disincentivizes work. The question is: What is the most likely economic outcome?
  2. The Clash of Human Dignity: The Government argues UBI provides a fundamental safety net that gives people autonomy and dignity. The Opposition argues that true dignity comes from work and contribution, which UBI undermines. The question is: What is the true source of individual dignity and self-worth?

A good summary speaker would structure their speech around these two clashes, explaining why their team wins both using the weighing mechanisms above.

Logical Fallacies

What are Logical Fallacies?

A logical fallacy is an error in reasoning that makes an argument invalid. They are surprisingly common in everyday conversation and debate. Learning to spot them is like gaining a superpower: you can identify weak points in your opponent's case and strengthen your own by avoiding these pitfalls.

1. Straw Man

What It Is: Misrepresenting or exaggerating your opponent's argument to make it easier to attack. Instead of debating their actual point, you attack a weaker, distorted version (the "straw man").

How to Avoid It: Always address the strongest, most charitable interpretation of your opponent's argument. Steel-man their case before you rebut it.

How to Spot It: Listen for phrases like "So what you're really saying is..." or "So you believe that..." followed by an extreme or simplistic version of your argument.

How to Use It Strategically: Call it out directly. "The opposition has built a straw man of our case. They claim we want to [distorted version], but what we actually argued was [your actual point]. Let's debate the real issue." This shows the judge you are the more reasonable and engaged team.

2. Ad Hominem

What It Is: Attacking the person making the argument, rather than the argument itself. This shifts the focus from the logic of the case to the character of the speaker.

How to Avoid It: Focus exclusively on the content of your opponent's speech. Their motivations, background, or character are irrelevant to the truth of their claims.

How to Spot It: Any time an opponent criticizes a speaker's personal traits ("You can't trust them, they're a politician") instead of their logic, it's an ad hominem.

How to Use It Strategically: "The opposition has spent a minute attacking our team instead of our arguments. This is an ad hominem fallacy. Let's ignore the personal attacks and return to the actual substance of this debate, which is..."

3. Slippery Slope

What It Is: Arguing that a small, initial step will inevitably lead to a chain of related, negative events without providing sufficient evidence for this chain reaction.

How to Avoid It: If you want to argue long-term consequences, you must prove each link in the causal chain. Explain the mechanism for why A leads to B, B leads to C, and so on.

How to Spot It: Listen for a series of escalating, unsupported claims. "If we allow A, then B will happen, and then C, and soon we'll have Z!"

How to Use It Strategically: "The opposition has presented a slippery slope fallacy. They have not proven why our policy would inevitably lead to these extreme outcomes. They've jumped from A to Z without any analysis of the steps in between. Let's focus on the probable, not the speculative."

4. False Dichotomy

What It Is: Presenting a situation as having only two possible options when, in fact, there are other alternatives. This is often an "either/or" argument that forces a choice between two extremes.

How to Avoid It: Acknowledge the nuance and complexity of the issue. Recognize that there may be middle ground or alternative solutions.

How to Spot It: When an opponent says, "We have to choose between X and Y," or "It's either our plan or chaos."

How to Use It Strategically: "The other team has presented a false dichotomy. They claim we must choose between their extreme policy and total disaster. This is not true. There is a third, more reasonable option, which is..." This allows you to introduce a more nuanced stance or a counter-model.

5. Hasty Generalization

What It Is: Drawing a broad conclusion based on a small or unrepresentative sample size. A single example is used to prove a general rule.

How to Avoid It: Use examples to illustrate a point you've already proven with logic, not as proof itself. If you use an example, explain why it's representative of a wider trend.

How to Spot It: When a speaker uses one anecdote to make a sweeping claim. "I know someone who did X, and it was terrible. Therefore, X is always terrible."

How to Use It Strategically: "The opposition's entire argument rests on a single anecdote. This is a hasty generalization. They haven't provided any structural reasons to believe this one case is representative of a broader trend. Our argument, on the other hand, is based on..."

6. Appeal to Authority

What It Is: Claiming something must be true simply because a person of authority (or a supposed expert) said it, without any other supporting evidence.

How to Avoid It: Use expert opinions as supporting evidence, but always explain the logic behind that opinion. The reasoning is more important than the person who said it.

How to Spot It: "As Nobel laureate Dr. X said..." or "The UN report states..." without any further explanation of the data or reasoning.

How to Use It Strategically: "While we respect Dr. X, simply stating their conclusion is an appeal to authority, not an argument. The opposition needs to explain the reasoning behind that conclusion for it to have weight in this debate. Without that, it's just an unsubstantiated claim."

7. Red Herring

What It Is: Introducing an irrelevant topic or piece of information to divert attention away from the central issue of the debate.

How to Avoid It: Stay focused on the core clashes of the debate. Ensure every point you make directly links back to the motion.

How to Spot It: When an opponent starts talking about an issue that is only tangentially related to the motion, especially in response to a difficult question or point.

How to Use It Strategically: "This is a red herring. The point about [irrelevant topic] may be interesting, but it does not address the central question of this debate, which is [the actual issue]. Let's get back on track." This shows you have control over the direction of the debate.

8. Circular Reasoning

What It Is: An argument that begins with the conclusion it's trying to prove. The premise and the conclusion are the same, just restated in different words.

How to Avoid It: Ensure your premises are independent of your conclusion. Your evidence should build towards your claim, not just restate it.

How to Spot It: "X is true because of Y, and Y is true because of X." For example, "This policy is good for the economy because it stimulates economic growth."

How to Use It Strategically: "The opposition's argument is circular. They've told us their policy is good because it's good, without providing any external mechanism or proof. This is an assertion, not an argument, and should be given no weight."
