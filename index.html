Debate Formats

Understanding Debate Formats

While the core principles of argumentation are universal, different debate formats have unique rules, roles, and strategies. This guide is primarily based on the World Schools Debating Championships (WSDC) format, as its 3-vs-3 structure and focus on clear, substantive debate provide a strong foundation for most other styles.

World Schools (WSDC)

A 3-vs-3 format where teams (Proposition and Opposition) have three speakers each giving 8-minute speeches. It emphasizes clear argumentation, engagement through POIs, and a holistic summary of the debate's key clashes. A key feature is the 4-minute Reply Speech, given by either the first or second speaker at the end of the debate, which frames the debate from their side's perspective.

British Parliamentary (BP)

A 4-team format with two teams on each side (Opening Government, Closing Government, Opening Opposition, Closing Opposition). Each team has two speakers. The challenge lies in defeating the opposing side while also distinguishing your team from your partner team on the same bench. BP requires strategic extensions and nuanced weighing.

American Parliamentary (AP)

A 2-vs-2 format with a Prime Minister (PM), Member of Opposition (MO), Minister of the Crown (MC), and Leader of the Opposition (LO). It features different speech times and a strong emphasis on rhetoric, framework debates, and direct refutation within a fast-paced structure.

1st Proposition

The Architect. Your job is to set up the debate. You must clearly define the motion, present your team's model (if needed), and lay out your case. You will use CPR to frame the debate and present 1-2 clear arguments using the PEEL structure. Your speech should be clear, confident, and leave no doubt about what your team stands for.

1st Opposition

The Architect of the Opposition. Your first job is to clash directly with the 1st Prop by rebutting their case setup and arguments. Then, you must present your own constructive case, often with its own CPR framing, and deliver your own arguments using PEEL. You must be both a rebuilder and a builder.

2nd Proposition & 2nd Opposition

The Builder & The Brawler. These speakers have two jobs. First, they must defend their own case and rebut the arguments made by the previous speaker. This is crucial for staying in the debate. Second, they must introduce new arguments to extend their case and add depth. These speeches are about both defense and offense.

3rd Proposition & 3rd Opposition

The Analyst. The third speakers do not introduce new arguments (unless explicitly flagged by their first speaker, which is rare and risky). Their entire speech is dedicated to rebuttal and analysis. Your job is to identify the main Points of Clash in the debate and explain why your team has won them. This requires you to listen carefully to the entire debate and synthesize the key issues.

Reply Speeches (Opp then Prop)

The Storyteller. This is a 4-minute speech given by the 1st or 2nd speaker. It's a biased summary of the debate from your side's perspective. You should not introduce new arguments or rebuttal. Instead, you should frame the debate around the key clashes and explain why, based on the arguments made, your team's vision of the world is more compelling and should win.

Scoring & Judging

The Role of the Judge

In debate, the judge is not an expert you need to impress with obscure knowledge. They are an average reasonable person. Your goal is to persuade them that your arguments are more logical, more important, and more relevant than your opponent's. They listen to the debate, take notes, and decide which team was more persuasive based on a clear set of criteria.

The Gold Standard: Sound Arguments

The most important part of judging is assessing the quality of your arguments. The best arguments are sound. To understand what this means, we need to know two key terms from logic: validity and soundness.

Validity

An argument is valid if its conclusion logically follows from its premises. The structure is correct. It doesn't matter if the premises are actually true, only that *if* they were true, the conclusion would have to be true as well.

Soundness

An argument is sound if it is both valid AND its premises are true. This is the goal. A sound argument is logically perfect and grounded in reality, making it extremely persuasive.

How to Check for Soundness: A Quick Guide

Before you make an argument, ask yourself these questions:

  • Is my claim (Point) clear and specific?
  • Is my reasoning (Explanation) logical? Does A really lead to B, and B to C?
  • Are my premises (the assumptions behind my explanation) true? Or at least, are they highly plausible to an average person?
  • Is my example relevant and does it make my reasoning more believable?

Examples: Valid, Sound, and Flawed Arguments

Invalid Argument

"All dogs are mammals. My cat is a mammal. Therefore, my cat is a dog."

Flaw: The structure is broken. Just because two things share one property (being a mammal), it doesn't make them the same. The conclusion does not logically follow from the premises.

Valid, but Unsound Argument

"All birds can fly. A penguin is a bird. Therefore, a penguin can fly."

Flaw: The structure is perfectly valid (If all A are B, and C is A, then C is B). However, the first premise, "All birds can fly," is factually false. Because a premise is untrue, the argument is unsound and unpersuasive.

Sound Argument

"All humans are mortal. Socrates is a human. Therefore, Socrates is mortal."

Success: The argument is valid in its structure, and both of its premises ("All humans are mortal" and "Socrates is a human") are true. This makes the conclusion undeniable. This is the quality you should aim for.

Scoring Categories (WSDC)

Judges in the World Schools format break down their scores into three categories. This ensures a holistic assessment of each speaker.

Content (40%)

This is the substance of your speech. It includes the quality of your arguments (are they sound?), the evidence you provide, and the effectiveness of your rebuttal. This is the most important category and is where logical rigor is rewarded.

Style (40%)

This is your delivery. It includes your tone of voice, speaking pace, eye contact, gestures, and overall confidence. Good style makes your content more persuasive and engaging for the judge. You can practice this in the Manner Lab.

Strategy (20%)

This is your structure and teamwork. It includes the organization of your speech (was it easy to follow?), your time management, and your team's overall strategic decisions. A clear strategy makes your content easier for the judge to understand and credit.

CPR & Framing

What is CPR?

CPR is your first step in prep time. It's a brainstorming framework that forces you to define the debate on your terms. By establishing the Context, identifying a clear Problem, and proposing a direct Resolution, you build a coherent case with a strong narrative from the very beginning. Spend the first 2-3 minutes of prep on this.

Context

This is the "reality" of the debate. What is happening in the world right now that makes this motion relevant? You need to paint a picture for the judge. Key questions: What is the status quo? What are the key facts and trends? Why are we debating this now?

Problem

Within your context, what is the specific harm or injustice that needs to be fixed? Who is the primary victim? Why is their suffering significant? A clear, compelling problem gives your case a moral core and a clear purpose.

Resolution

This is your team's ultimate goal or aim. For problem-based motions (like "THW..."), your resolution is how you solve the problem. For comparative motions (like "THR..." or "THBT..."), your resolution is the better world you are advocating for. This is the core idea that every argument you make should link back to in the PEEL framework.

Framing with Emotive Language

Framing is not just about stating facts; it's about painting a picture. The words you choose for your CPR can make the judge feel the urgency of the problem. Using emotive language helps you characterize the status quo in a way that makes your solution seem not just logical, but necessary and just.

Good vs. Bad CPR

THW ban unpaid internships.

Good Example

C: In a world of crippling student debt and soaring living costs, the dream of a successful career is increasingly accessible only to a privileged few who can afford to work for free. P: This creates an exploitative system that slams the door on talented, low-income students, entrenching generational inequality and robbing industries of the diverse perspectives they desperately need. R: Our resolution is to shatter this glass ceiling by banning unpaid internships, ensuring that opportunity is dictated by merit, not money.

Bad Example

C: Internships are a thing companies offer. P: It's unfair that some people don't get paid. R: We would ban them so everyone gets paid.

(Why it's bad: Vague, unemotional, and fails to create a compelling narrative for the judge.)

Pro Tip: Build Your Word Bank

The best debaters have a rich vocabulary of persuasive phrases. Build yours by actively listening to skilled speakers (politicians, activists, lawyers) and reading widely. When you hear a powerful phrase, write it down. Soon you'll have a toolkit of words to frame any debate.

PEEL: Point, Explain, Example, Link

What is PEEL?

PEEL is the universal structure for a strong, persuasive argument. It's the atomic unit of debate. Following this structure ensures your points are clear, logical, and impactful, guiding the judge through your reasoning step-by-step and making it hard to refute.

Point

State your argument in a single, clear, and concise sentence. This is the headline for your argument.

Explain

Provide the logical reason why your point is true. This is the "mechanism" of your argument. Ask yourself "why?" multiple times to deepen your analysis. Explain the incentives of actors and the chain of cause and effect.

Example

Give a specific, real-world example or a well-reasoned hypothetical that illustrates your explanation in action. An example doesn't prove your point, but it makes your explanation more plausible and memorable.

Link

Connect your argument back to the core question of the debate. Explain why this specific point is important and how it helps your side win. This is where you connect back to your team's overall aim, as defined in the Resolution of your CPR.

Good vs. Bad PEEL

THBT standardized testing is more harmful than beneficial.

Good Example

P: Standardized testing perpetuates educational inequality. E: Because these tests are expensive to prepare for, wealthy families can afford extensive tutoring and prep courses, giving their children a significant advantage. This transforms the test from a measure of aptitude into a measure of wealth. E: For instance, the SAT has been shown to have a strong correlation with family income; students from the highest income brackets consistently score hundreds of points higher than those from the lowest. L: Therefore, by relying on these tests, we are not creating a meritocracy, but are actively entrenching the privileges of the wealthy, which is the most significant harm in a debate about fairness in education.

Bad Example

P: Standardized tests are bad. E: They don't really test intelligence. E: The SAT is a bad test. L: So we should get rid of them.

(Why it's bad: The point is a vague assertion, the explanation lacks a mechanism, the example is just a name-drop, and the link has no weighing.)

Stakeholders

What is Stakeholder Analysis?

Debates are about people. Stakeholder analysis is the process of identifying the key groups affected by a motion and analyzing their interests and incentives. This is one of the best ways to find the most important arguments in a round.

How to Use a Stakeholder Matrix

A stakeholder matrix helps you organize your thoughts. For each stakeholder, you should identify their current situation, their core interests, how the motion impacts them, and why that impact is important.

The NILE Framework

What is NILE?

NILE helps you build a diverse and robust case by prompting four different kinds of arguments. Instead of making four similar points, NILE pushes you to argue from different philosophical angles, making your case more resilient and comprehensive.

Necessity

Why must we act now? This argument establishes urgency. It proves that the status quo is not just bad, but actively harmful or getting worse. It answers the "why now?" question, making the debate feel important.

Impact

What are the long-term consequences? This focuses on the scale and significance of the outcome. It explains how your policy will lead to the best possible future by creating broad, positive changes for society, the economy, or the environment.

Legitimacy

What principle justifies this? This is an argument from moral philosophy. It claims your side is right based on a core value like fairness, autonomy, rights, or justice, regardless of the practical outcomes. It shows your side is morally consistent.

Effectiveness

How will this change behavior? This is a practical argument about incentives. It explains how the motion will work in the real world by altering the behavior of key stakeholders in a predictable and beneficial way. It proves your plan is plausible.

NILE Example

THBT social media has done more harm than good.

  • Necessity: The current trajectory of teen mental health, which is strongly correlated with social media use, constitutes a crisis that demands immediate action to avert further harm.
  • Impact: In the long term, the algorithmic promotion of outrage over nuance erodes the shared factual reality required for a functioning democracy, an impact that outweighs any connective benefits.
  • Legitimacy: The business model of social media, predicated on harvesting vast amounts of personal data without meaningful consent, is a fundamental violation of the right to privacy.
  • Effectiveness: The platforms are designed to be addictive, changing human behavior to prioritize short-term validation over genuine connection, which is a predictable and harmful effect of their core mechanics.

MICE Incentives

What is MICE?

MICE is a framework for analyzing why individuals and groups act the way they do. When you analyze a stakeholder, you can use MICE to predict their behavior by understanding their core motivations. This makes your analysis deeper and more persuasive.

Money

The drive for financial gain, economic security, and material well-being. This is often the most straightforward incentive for corporations and individuals in economic debates.

Ideology

The drive to act in accordance with one's beliefs, values, and worldview. This can be political, religious, or moral, and often explains actions that seem to run counter to material self-interest.

Coercion

The drive to act based on fear or external pressure. An actor is coerced when they act to avoid a negative consequence, such as punishment, social ostracism, or physical harm.

Ego

The drive for personal fulfillment, respect, pride, and a sense of purpose. This isn't just about arrogance; it's about the deep human need to feel valued and maintain a positive self-image.

MICE Example

THW hold social media platforms legally liable for harmful user-generated content.

Analysis of a Social Media CEO's incentives:

  • Money: The CEO's primary incentive is to maximize profit. They will resist this motion because content moderation at the scale required is extremely expensive and would reduce profits. Their compensation is tied to stock performance, which is tied to user engagement, and controversial content is often highly engaging.
  • Ideology: The CEO may genuinely believe in a libertarian interpretation of "free speech," arguing that their platform is a neutral public square and should not be the arbiter of truth. They will frame their opposition to the motion as a defense of this principle.
  • Coercion: If the law passes, the CEO will be forced to invest heavily in moderation to avoid massive fines and lawsuits. The fear of legal and financial punishment becomes a powerful coercive incentive to change their behavior and police their platform.
  • Ego: The CEO's self-image is tied to being seen as an innovator and a champion of free expression. They will be motivated to fight the motion to protect this legacy and avoid the public perception of being a censor or a purveyor of harmful content.

Layering

What is Layering?

Layering is an advanced analytical technique that involves breaking down broad categories into more specific, nuanced sub-groups. Instead of treating a group like "citizens" or an issue like "the economy" as a single entity, layering allows you to explore how a motion affects different parts of that group or issue in different ways. This creates more sophisticated and persuasive analysis.

Layering by Stakeholder

The most common form of layering is to break down a large stakeholder group. A single group is never a monolith; it contains diverse interests. By analyzing these sub-groups, you can uncover unique impacts.

Example: THW ban smoking.

A simple analysis looks at "citizens." A layered analysis breaks this down:

  • Smokers vs. Non-Smokers: The ban directly impacts the autonomy of smokers but protects non-smokers from secondhand smoke. This creates a direct clash of rights.
  • Rich Smokers vs. Poor Smokers: A wealthy smoker might easily switch to expensive alternatives like vaping or therapy. A low-income smoker, for whom smoking might be a primary stress-relief mechanism, is disproportionately harmed by the ban, which can feel paternalistic and regressive.
  • Young People vs. Older Adults: The ban might be most effective at preventing young people from ever starting, a huge long-term health benefit. For older, lifelong smokers, the ban might be less effective and cause significant distress.

Layering by Issue

You can also apply layering to the issues themselves. Instead of just talking about "the effect on citizens," you can break down the different ways they are affected.

Example: Analyzing the "Low-Income Smoker" from the motion THW ban smoking.

  • Health Layer: How does the ban affect physical health (cancer rates) and mental health (stress, loss of a coping mechanism)?
  • Wealth Layer: How does it affect an individual's finances? (Money saved from not buying cigarettes vs. potential costs of black market products or cessation aids). How does it affect the nation's wealth? (Reduced healthcare costs vs. lost tax revenue).
  • Social Life Layer: How does it affect social interactions? (End of the "smokers' corner" at work, potential for social stigma or black market communities).

By layering your analysis, you can generate multiple, distinct arguments from a single starting point, making your case far richer and more difficult to refute.

Maslow's Hierarchy of Needs

What is Maslow's Hierarchy?

Developed by psychologist Abraham Maslow, this framework suggests that human needs are arranged in a hierarchy. People are motivated to fulfill basic needs before moving on to more advanced needs. In debate, this is an incredibly powerful tool for weighing impacts. It allows you to argue that your impacts are more fundamental, and therefore more important, than your opponent's.

The Hierarchy of Needs

How to Use it in a Debate

The core use of Maslow's Hierarchy is to prove your impacts are more important. You can argue that a policy that secures a lower-level need (like safety) must be prioritized over a policy that only affects a higher-level need (like esteem).

Example: THW abolish the Olympic Games.

  • Opposition's Argument: "The Olympics must stay because they provide a huge source of national pride and a sense of global community."
    (This argument operates at the level of Esteem and Love & Belonging.)
  • Your (Proposition) Weighing: "The opposition talks about feelings of pride and community. We concede that these are nice to have. However, our argument is that hosting the Olympics cripples host cities with debt, forcing them to cut funding for hospitals, schools, and policing. This directly threatens the Physiological and Safety needs of their poorest citizens. According to Maslow's hierarchy, you cannot achieve esteem or belonging if your basic survival and security are at risk. Therefore, protecting citizens from tangible harm must be prioritized over the intangible benefit of national pride, and our impact is fundamentally more important."

Logical Fallacies

What is a Logical Fallacy?

A logical fallacy is a flaw in reasoning. They are like tricks or illusions of thought, and they're often used to make an argument seem stronger than it really is. Spotting them in your opponent's case is a powerful form of rebuttal, and avoiding them in your own case makes your arguments more sound and persuasive.

8 Common Fallacies in Debate

1. Strawman

Definition: Misrepresenting or exaggerating your opponent's argument to make it easier to attack.

Example: "My opponent wants to regulate the industry. So, they want the government to control every aspect of our lives and destroy all businesses."

Why it's a fallacy: You're not refuting their actual argument, but a weaker, distorted version you created.

2. Slippery Slope

Definition: Asserting that if we allow A to happen, then Z will consequently happen too, without providing evidence that Z is the inevitable consequence of A.

Example: "If we allow students to retake one test, they'll soon expect to retake every test, and then they'll never study at all."

Why it's a fallacy: It avoids engaging with the actual issue by shifting attention to extreme, hypothetical outcomes.

3. False Dichotomy

Definition: Presenting two alternative states as the only possibilities, when in fact more possibilities exist.

Example: "You're either with us, or you're against us. There is no middle ground."

Why it's a fallacy: It artificially limits the options to create a false choice, ignoring nuance and other valid alternatives.

4. Circular Argument

Definition: An argument that begins with what it's trying to end with. The conclusion is used as one of the premises.

Example: "This policy is the best because it is superior to all other policies."

Why it's a fallacy: It doesn't actually prove anything; it just restates the claim in a different way without any evidence.

5. Appeal to Authority

Definition: Claiming something is true because an authority figure or expert said it, without any other supporting evidence.

Example: "Well, a famous Nobel Prize-winning physicist said this economic theory is correct, so it must be."

Why it's a fallacy: The expert could be wrong, or speaking outside their field of expertise. The argument should stand on its own logic.

6. Bandwagon

Definition: Appealing to popularity or the fact that many people do something as an attempted form of validation.

Example: "Millions of people believe in this, so it must be true."

Why it's a fallacy: The popularity of an idea has no bearing on its validity.

7. Hasty Generalization

Definition: Drawing a conclusion based on a small sample size, rather than looking at statistics that are much more in line with the typical or average situation.

Example: "I met two people from that city and they were both rude. Therefore, everyone from that city is rude."

Why it's a fallacy: The sample size is insufficient to support the broad conclusion.

8. Appeal to Emotion

Definition: Manipulating an emotional response in place of a valid or compelling argument.

Example: "Think of the children! We must pass this law to protect them, regardless of the costs or consequences."

Why it's a fallacy: While emotion can be a powerful persuasive tool, it's not a substitute for logical reasoning.

Bookends

What are Bookends?

Bookends are a powerful stylistic device where a speaker begins their speech with a memorable story, quote, or analogy and then returns to it in their conclusion. This creates a satisfying, cohesive narrative for the judge, making the speech feel complete and well-planned. A good bookend makes your core message stick.

How to Create Effective Bookends

The key is to find a central theme for your speech and encapsulate it in a memorable package. This can be:

  • A Metaphor or Analogy: A powerful image that represents the core of your case (e.g., a "house of cards," a "leaky boat," a "garden that needs tending").
  • A Quote: A famous or poignant quote that captures the spirit of your argument.
  • An Anecdote: A short, personal story (real or hypothetical) that illustrates the problem or solution you're discussing.

Bookend Examples

1. The Metaphor

Motion: THW ban social media for under-16s.

Opening: "Madam Speaker, imagine a beautiful, vibrant garden, full of young plants... but a chemical company starts selling a new, addictive fertilizer that stunts their roots... That garden is our society of young people, and that fertilizer is social media. We are here today to get that poison out of the garden."

Closing: "So, at the end of this debate... The opposition has asked us to keep spraying this addictive chemical on our garden... We ask you to make a simple choice: let the garden wither, or give it a chance to grow strong and healthy on its own. Choose to get the poison out of the garden."

2. The Quote

Motion: THS the principle of "my body, my choice."

Opening: "The philosopher John Stuart Mill once wrote, 'Over himself, over his own body and mind, the individual is sovereign.' This is the foundational principle of a free society, and it is the principle we stand here to defend today."

Closing: "The other side has given you a world of state control, where personal decisions are subject to government approval. We have defended a world of individual liberty. We ask you to stand with Mill, to stand with freedom, and to affirm that over their own body and mind, the individual must remain sovereign."

3. The Anecdote

Motion: THW implement a Universal Basic Income.

Opening: "Let me tell you about Sarah. She's a single mother working two jobs, but one unexpected car repair, one sick child, means she's on the brink of eviction. Her life is a tightrope walk with no safety net. The motion today is about giving millions of people like Sarah that safety net."

Closing: "Remember Sarah. The opposition has dismissed her struggles as a personal failure. They've offered her nothing but the same broken system. We offered her a floor beneath the tightrope. We offered her dignity. By passing this motion, we choose to build a world where one piece of bad luck doesn't have to mean ruin. We choose to catch Sarah."

Rebuttal

What is Rebuttal?

Rebuttal is the art of dismantling your opponent's arguments. It's not enough to present your own case; you must also show why the other side is wrong. Good rebuttal directly engages with the opponent's logic and proves it to be flawed, irrelevant, or less important than your own points.

How to Structure Rebuttal

Use a simple, three-step process for every rebuttal point:

  1. "They said..." - State their argument clearly and concisely.
  2. "But..." - Explain exactly why their argument is wrong. (e.g., logical flaw, incorrect fact, mischaracterization).
  3. "Therefore..." - Explain the implication. Why does this rebuttal matter? How does it weaken their case and strengthen yours?

Good vs. Bad Rebuttal

Opponent's Argument: "A tax on sugary drinks is regressive and unfairly harms the poor."

Good Rebuttal

"They said this tax unfairly harms the poor. But this argument ignores that the health consequences of obesity and diabetes, like lost wages and high medical bills, are also disproportionately borne by the poor. While our policy has a short-term financial cost, their world locks the poor into a long-term cycle of poor health and poverty. Therefore, our policy is actually more progressive because it addresses a root cause of inequality, making it the more compassionate choice."

Bad Rebuttal

"They said this hurts the poor, but that's not true. Poor people should just drink healthier things. This argument is wrong."

(Why it's bad: It's an assertion, not an argument. It dismisses the point without engaging its logic and offers no counter-analysis or weighing.)

Hierarchy of Disagreement

What is the Hierarchy of Disagreement?

Adapted from Paul Graham's essay, this hierarchy provides a model for understanding the quality of rebuttal. It ranks forms of disagreement from the weakest (personal attacks) to the strongest (refuting the central point). In a debate, your goal is to always operate at the highest levels of the hierarchy. A judge will not be persuaded by low-level disagreement.

In WSDC, any rebuttal at levels DH0, DH1, or DH2 is considered poor form and will score very low. Effective rebuttal begins at DH3.

DH0: Name-Calling

The lowest form of disagreement. This is a direct personal insult with no substance.
Example: "That's a stupid argument made by an idiot."

DH1: Ad Hominem

Attacking the speaker's characteristics or authority instead of their argument.
Example: "Of course he would argue for lower taxes, he's a wealthy businessman."

DH2: Responding to Tone

Criticizing the way an argument was made, rather than the argument itself.
Example: "I can't believe the speaker was so arrogant and dismissive when they made that point."

DH3: Contradiction

Stating the opposing case with little or no supporting evidence. It's simply asserting the opposite is true.
Example: "The speaker claimed this policy would create jobs, but that's not true. It will actually destroy jobs."

DH4: Counterargument

Contradicting and then backing it up with reasoning and evidence. This is the first level of truly persuasive rebuttal.
Example: "They said this will create jobs, but that's unlikely because the policy raises taxes on small businesses, which studies show are the primary drivers of job growth."

DH5: Refutation

Finding a specific error in the opponent's argument and explaining why that error invalidates their point. This requires quoting them directly.
Example: "The speaker's entire jobs argument was based on a 2015 study. However, they failed to mention that a more comprehensive 2022 study completely refutes those findings, showing a net job loss. Their evidence is outdated and their conclusion is wrong."

DH6: Refuting the Central Point

Explicitly identifying the opponent's main argument and defeating it. This is the most powerful form of disagreement.
Example: "The core of the opposition's case is the idea that economic efficiency is the most important goal. We refute this central premise. In a just society, the dignity and well-being of citizens must come before profit. By winning this clash of principles, we show that even if their policy were efficient, it would still be morally wrong."

Points of Information (POIs)

What are POIs?

A Point of Information is a short question or statement (under 15 seconds) offered to an opponent during their speech. They are a crucial tool for engagement, allowing you to interact directly during constructive speeches. You should aim to offer several POIs during each opponent's speech and accept 1-2.

The Four Types of POIs

Clarification: Ask for a definition or detail if the speaker is being vague. "On that, could you clarify what you mean by 'a significant investment'?"
Challenge: Directly question the logic or evidence of their point. "On that, if your plan is so effective, why has it failed in the real-world example of X?"
Contradiction: Point out an inconsistency with something their partner said. "On that, your partner said this policy was about freedom, but you're arguing it's about safety. Which is it?"
Setup: Ask a seemingly innocent question that sets up an argument your partner will make later. "On that, would you agree that the primary duty of a state is to protect its most vulnerable citizens?"

Strategy for Offering and Answering POIs

Offering POIs
  • Be Concise: Keep it under 15 seconds. A long POI is not a POI; it's a speech, and the speaker will rightly wave it down.
  • Be Persistent, Not Annoying: Stand up and offer POIs regularly, but don't engage in "barracking" (shouting or making noise). A calm "Point of Information" or "On that point" is sufficient.
  • Target Weaknesses: Offer a POI when the speaker seems unsure, is making a weak argument, or is transitioning between points. A well-timed POI can disrupt their flow.
Answering POIs
  • Take Two: Aim to accept two POIs during a constructive speech. Taking none makes you look scared; taking too many can derail your speech.
  • Stay in Control: You decide when to take a POI. A good time is after you've finished a point. Say "Yes, I'll take you now" or "One moment."
  • Answer Briefly and Pivot: Answer the question directly and concisely, then immediately explain why it doesn't damage your case or, even better, how it reinforces your point. Then, move on. Don't let the POI drag you into a two-minute back-and-forth.

Points of Clash

What are Clashes?

Clashes are the main points of disagreement in the debate. They are the fundamental questions that the debate boils down to. Identifying these clashes allows you to structure your later speeches (especially the summary/whip speech) around the most important issues, showing the judge why you win the arguments that matter most. A debate isn't won by winning the most arguments, but by winning the most *important* arguments.

How to Identify and Win Clashes

1. Identify the Core Questions

After the first few speeches, take a step back and ask: "What are the two or three fundamental disagreements here?" Don't just list the arguments. Group them thematically. Often, clashes are about:

  • Principles vs. Practicality: Is there a moral right/wrong at stake, and does it outweigh the practical outcomes?
  • Economic Impact vs. Social Impact: Does the financial benefit outweigh the harm to community or culture?
  • Short-Term vs. Long-Term: Is it better to solve an immediate crisis or build a more stable future?
  • Freedom vs. Security: How much individual liberty should be sacrificed for collective safety?

2. Win the Clash with Weighing

Once you identify a clash, you need to prove you've won it. You do this through weighing: directly comparing your impacts to your opponent's and showing why yours are more significant. Use these mechanisms:

  • Magnitude/Scale: "Our impact affects millions of people, while theirs only affects a small, privileged group."
  • Severity: "Their benefit is a minor convenience; our harm is a matter of life and death."
  • Likelihood: "Our harm is a direct, proven consequence of their policy. Their benefit is based on a chain of optimistic assumptions that are unlikely to happen."
  • Timeframe: "We must address the immediate crisis our policy solves before we can even begin to think about their long-term, speculative goals."
  • Vulnerability: "Even if their impact is widespread, our policy protects the most vulnerable and marginalized group in this debate, and the primary duty of the state is to protect those who cannot protect themselves."
